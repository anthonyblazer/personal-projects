Introduction

During the summer of 2023, I decided to embark on many forms of self-improvement. Forms included reading, starting a new diet, investing, learning more Computer Science languages, and ultimately, broadening my musical knowledge. In doing this, I decided to listen to a new album every gym session (so five times a week), all some of the highest rated albums of all time. These albums have ranged in genres from Rap, Hip-Hop, Rock, Alternative Rock, Blues, and more. Included on this webpage are my personal ratings and thoughts on the albums. If you agree, that's great, and if you don't, then that it the beauty of music. It is subjective and we recieve it differently than others.

Before you start, read the following points and keep them in mind while reading. Pleasant reading!

  • These albums are greatly compared to each other. So a 8.7 is an 8.7 in comparison to the 9.5's and so on. This is important because a 6/10 album may be 6/10 compared to the other albums components like lyricism, production, and etc, but I may thoroughly enjoy it outside of this ranking.
  • Additionally, ratings and ranking may change since I am continuing learning the backstories of the albums included, and the artists who made them.

Graceland by Paul Simon

9.6/10

Best Songs:

  1. Homeless (with LadySmith Black Mambazo)
  2. Graceland
  3. Diamonds On The Soles Of Her Shoes

Honorable Mentions:

  1. I Know What I Know
  2. You Can Call Me Al

Initial Thoughts:

This was my final album in my musical summer experience, and it was one the strongest albums I have listened to since the beginning of my journey! This album did so many things right, but to show those, you need to understand the history of this album. This album was a rejuvenating project for Paul Simon, who had been battling depression, divorce, and a lackluster previous album. This album was created during the time of the horrific Apartheid, which meant that it broke boycotts from the U.S. on African connections. This album encompassed U.S. based styles in Pop and Classic Rock, but introduced the styles of Mbaqanga, folk, and Afro pop. Every song has its own style, with some like Homeless and Under African Skies giving the African musicians he teamed up with the opportunities to shine. The lyrics all had messages behind them, like the results of Apartheid (Homeless), foreign influence and impressions (You Can Call Me Al), the misuse of riches (Diamonds On The Sole of Her Shoes), and more. In my mind, this album had flaws only in the progression of the music.

8.8/10

  2. One of Us Must Know

Honorable Mentions:

  1. Sad Eye Woman of the Lowlands
  2. I Want You

Initial Thoughts:

This album was one of the biggest change in styles that I have seen from a Rock album. While most rock albums have slight deviations but still hold the traditional instrumentals, which is not a bad thing at all, this album almost ditched standard electric instrumentals for more non traditional instruments like standard guitar, tambourines, and harmonicas. For storytelling, this album was with those that have simple storytelling, with memorable ones being Stuck in Mobile with the Memphis Blues, Leopard Skin Pill Box Hat, and Fourth Time Around. The cherry on top of this album was Bob Dylan’s voice. Bob Dylan’s voice is a higher pitched voice with slight raspiness, but it worked flawlessly with the albums instrumentals and country-like feel.
